RF Small-Signal Analysis
of a GaN HEMT

A complete walkthrough of the theory behind 2-port AC simulation — from Y-parameters and S-parameters to fT, fmax, and Mason's U — tied directly to your Sentaurus TCAD data and plotting script.

01 The Big Picture

When you run an AC simulation in Sentaurus TCAD, you are linearising the transistor around a DC operating point (a specific VGS, VDS) and sweeping a small sinusoidal signal across frequency. The simulator solves the semiconductor equations in the frequency domain and outputs admittance (Y) parameters — complex numbers that describe how currents at each port respond to voltages at each port, at every frequency.

From these raw Y-parameters, everything else is derived:

Your CSV has 525 rows: 21 gate biases × 25 frequency points, each carrying four Y-parameter entries (Y₁₁, Y₁₂, Y₂₁, Y₂₂) split into conductance and capacitance columns. The script reads this, computes S-parameters, current gain, Mason's U, and extracts the key RF figures of merit.

02 GaN HEMT Basics

A High Electron Mobility Transistor (HEMT) uses a heterojunction — typically AlGaN/GaN — to confine electrons in a two-dimensional electron gas (2DEG) at the interface. The key properties that matter for RF:

  • High electron mobility in the 2DEG channel → high transconductance gm
  • Wide bandgap (3.4 eV for GaN) → high breakdown voltage, enabling high VDS
  • High saturation velocity → high fT

The device is treated as a 2-port network: port 1 is the gate (input), port 2 is the drain (output), and the source is the common terminal.

Port 1 (Gate): V₁ = V_GS, I₁ = I_G Port 2 (Drain): V₂ = V_DS, I₂ = I_D

03 The Small-Signal Equivalent Circuit

At a given DC bias, the transistor's behaviour for small AC perturbations is captured by lumped elements:

ElementSymbolPhysical Origin
Gate-source capacitanceCgsDepletion region under the gate
Gate-drain capacitanceCgdFeedback through the gate-drain overlap
Drain-source capacitanceCdsChannel/substrate coupling
TransconductancegmChannel current response to gate voltage
Output conductancegdsFinite slope of ID–VDS in saturation

The AC simulation effectively "measures" the full frequency-dependent network parameters that embed all these elements (plus distributed effects, parasitics, and non-quasi-static behaviour that simple lumped models miss).

04 Y-Parameters (Admittance Parameters)

Definition & the 2-Port Matrix

For a linear 2-port, the relationship between port currents and voltages is:

I₁ = Y₁₁ · V₁ + Y₁₂ · V₂ I₂ = Y₂₁ · V₁ + Y₂₂ · V₂

In matrix form:

Y₁₁
Y₁₂
Y₂₁
Y₂₂

Each element has a clear physical meaning:

ParameterMeasurement conditionPhysical meaning
Y₁₁I₁/V₁ with V₂ = 0Input admittance (≈ jωCgs + jωCgd at mid-freq)
Y₁₂I₁/V₂ with V₁ = 0Reverse transfer (≈ −jωCgd)
Y₂₁I₂/V₁ with V₂ = 0Forward transfer (≈ gm − jωCgd)
Y₂₂I₂/V₂ with V₁ = 0Output admittance (≈ gds + jωCds)

Every Yij is complex and frequency-dependent: it has a real part (conductance, G) and an imaginary part (susceptance, B = ωC).

Sentaurus Output Format

Sentaurus doesn't output a single complex number per Y-parameter. Instead, it decomposes each into two real columns:

CSV ColumnMeaningSymbol
a(i:j)Conductance (real part of Yij)Gij = Re(Yij)
c(i:j)Capacitance extracted from imaginary partCij = Im(Yij) / ω

Your CSV columns map as:

ColumnParameterTypical role
a11, c11Y₁₁ → G₁₁, C₁₁Input admittance → Cgs + Cgd
a12, c12Y₁₂ → G₁₂, C₁₂Reverse transfer → −Cgd
a21, c21Y₂₁ → G₂₁, C₂₁Forward transfer → gm
a22, c22Y₂₂ → G₂₂, C₂₂Output admittance → Cds

Reconstructing the Full Complex Y

To get the actual complex admittance used in all subsequent calculations:

Y_ij(f) = a(i:j) + j · 2π f · c(i:j) fundamental reconstruction
This is the critical step the script misses. It uses only a(i:j) (the real conductance) as if it were the full complex Y-parameter. Without the jωC imaginary term, all derived quantities (H₂₁, S-parameters, fT) are incorrect.

05 S-Parameters (Scattering Parameters)

What & Why

At RF/microwave frequencies, it's impractical to measure open-circuit voltages or short-circuit currents. Instead, we measure incident and reflected travelling waves at each port, normalised to a reference impedance Z₀ (typically 50 Ω).

S-parameterMeaning
S₁₁Input reflection coefficient (how much signal bounces back from port 1)
S₂₁Forward transmission (gain or loss from port 1 to port 2)
S₁₂Reverse transmission (isolation, feedback)
S₂₂Output reflection coefficient

Magnitudes are expressed in dB: |S₂₁|dB = 20 log₁₀|S₂₁|. This is what Plot ① in the script shows.

Y → S Conversion

Given complex Y-parameters and Z₀ = 50 Ω, define Y₀ = 1/Z₀:

Δ = (1 + Y₁₁·Z₀)(1 + Y₂₂·Z₀) − Y₁₂·Y₂₁·Z₀² S₁₁ = [(1 − Y₁₁·Z₀)(1 + Y₂₂·Z₀) + Y₁₂·Y₂₁·Z₀²] / Δ S₂₁ = −2·Y₂₁·Z₀ / Δ S₁₂ = −2·Y₁₂·Z₀ / Δ S₂₂ = [(1 + Y₁₁·Z₀)(1 − Y₂₂·Z₀) + Y₁₂·Y₂₁·Z₀²] / Δ

The script implements this in Y_to_S(). With correct complex Y, this gives physically meaningful S-parameters that can be plotted on a Smith chart (Plot ⑦) or as magnitude vs frequency (Plot ①).

06 RF Gain Definitions

Current Gain |H₂₁|

H₂₁ is the short-circuit current gain — the ratio of output current to input current when the output is AC-shorted. For a 2-port described by Y-parameters:

H₂₁ = −Y₂₁ / Y₁₁ simplified form

The script uses a more general formula derived from the H-parameter (hybrid parameter) matrix relationship:

H₂₁ = −2·Y₂₁ / [(1 − Y₁₁)(1 + Y₂₂) + Y₁₂·Y₂₁] script's formula

For a typical FET where |Yij| ≪ 1 (in siemens), the denominator ≈ 1, and this reduces to H₂₁ ≈ −2Y₂₁. However, this approximation assumes Y values are small compared to 1 S — which they are for your device (Y ~ 10⁻¹⁰ S).

Expected behaviour (with correct complex Y)

  • At low frequency: |H₂₁| ≈ gm / (ωCgs) — rises as frequency decreases (or is flat if well above a pole)
  • Rolls off at −20 dB/decade (−6 dB/octave) above the dominant pole
  • Crosses 0 dB at f = fT

Mason's Unilateral Power Gain U

Mason's U is the maximum power gain achievable when the device is unilateralised (feedback neutralised). It's an invariant — independent of embedding network — and is defined as:

|Y₂₁ − Y₁₂|² U = ───────────────────────────────────────── 4 · [Re(Y₁₁)·Re(Y₂₂) − Re(Y₁₂)·Re(Y₂₁)]

Key properties:

  • Also rolls off at −20 dB/decade at high frequencies
  • Crosses 0 dB at f = fmax
  • U ≥ |H₂₁|² always (since U accounts for power matching, not just current)
  • U is the ultimate frequency limit of the transistor — beyond fmax, no passive network can extract power gain
Why U looked reasonable despite the bug: The numerator |Y₂₁ − Y₁₂|² uses the magnitude of the difference, which doesn't strictly need the imaginary part to give a nonzero result (the real conductances alone produce a finite difference). The denominator uses only Re(Yij) terms explicitly. So the formula coincidentally produces plausible-looking values from purely real inputs — but they are not correct.

07 Figures of Merit: fT and fmax

Transit Frequency fT

fT is the frequency at which the short-circuit current gain |H₂₁| drops to unity (0 dB). Physically, it represents the maximum frequency at which the transistor can amplify current.

From the simplified small-signal model:

f_T = g_m / (2π · C_gs) first-order approximation

More precisely (including Cgd):

f_T = g_m / [2π · (C_gs + C_gd)] with feedback capacitance

fT depends on bias: it rises with gm (which increases with VGS until saturation effects set in), then drops when gm compresses or capacitances increase at high gate bias.

Maximum Oscillation Frequency fmax

fmax is the frequency where Mason's U drops to 0 dB — the absolute upper frequency for power gain. It depends on fT plus parasitic resistances:

f_T f_max ≈ ──────────────────────────────── 2 · √(R_g · g_ds + 2π·f_T·R_g·C_gd)

This is why fmax is always ≥ fT in practice (U rolls off slower than H₂₁). Gate resistance Rg is a major limiter of fmax.

Extraction by 0 dB Crossing

In the script, fT and fmax are extracted numerically from the gain-vs-frequency curves:

  1. Scan the gain array for the first index where gain goes from ≥ 0 dB to < 0 dB
  2. Linearly interpolate in log-frequency space to find the exact 0 dB crossing
  3. If the gain never reaches 0 dB (as with the buggy H₂₁ at −190 dB), extrapolate from the last point assuming −20 dB/dec slope
log₁₀(f₀) = log₁₀(f₁) + G₁ · [log₁₀(f₂) − log₁₀(f₁)] / (G₁ − G₂) linear interp in log-f

This is what extrapolate_cutoff() implements. For your data, H₂₁ never reaches 0 dB (stuck at −190 dB), so the fallback extrapolation gives fT ≈ 0.001 GHz — essentially zero.

08 Capacitances

The script extracts capacitances from the lowest-frequency row for each VGS, treating it as the quasi-static (DC) limit. The mapping from CSV columns to device capacitances is:

C_gs ≈ c(1:1) → c11 column (input capacitance) C_gd ≈ |c(1:2)| → |c12| column (feedback capacitance, sign-flipped) C_ds ≈ c(2:2) → c22 column (output capacitance)

These are converted to femtofarads (×10¹⁵) for plotting. Capacitance is bias-dependent: Cgs changes with gate depletion width, Cgd changes with the drain-side depletion edge, and both affect RF performance through fT.

Why lowest frequency? At f → 0, the imaginary part of Y is purely capacitive (B = ωC, so C = B/ω). The capacitance column in Sentaurus is already extracted this way, and it should be nearly frequency-independent for a well-behaved device. Using the lowest-frequency row minimizes non-quasi-static errors.

09 Transconductance gm

Transconductance is the small-signal current gain of the channel:

g_m = ∂I_D / ∂V_GS |_{V_DS=const} ≈ Re(Y₂₁) at low frequency

The script extracts |a21| at the lowest frequency for each VGS and converts to mS (×10³). gm should peak somewhere in the active bias range and then drop (gain compression). This is what Plot ⑤ shows.

In your CSV, a21 at 100 MHz ranges from ~1.25 × 10⁻¹⁰ to ~1.7 × 10⁻¹⁰ S (~0.17 nS). These are extremely small values — typical for a nm-scale simulation device that may need width-normalisation.

11 The Bug in the Script

The core issue is in compute_fom():

# What the script does (WRONG):
Y11 = sub["a11"].values.astype(complex)   # just the real conductance, cast to complex
Y12 = sub["a12"].values.astype(complex)
Y21 = sub["a21"].values.astype(complex)
Y22 = sub["a22"].values.astype(complex)

This gives Y₂₁ ≈ 1.5 × 10⁻¹⁰ + 0j (pure real, ≈ 0.15 nS). With the denominator ≈ 1, H₂₁ ≈ −2 × 1.5 × 10⁻¹⁰ ≈ 3 × 10⁻¹⁰, which is −190 dB. Completely unphysical.

What happens to each quantity:

S-parametersY·Z₀ ≈ 10⁻⁸ (S ≈ identity)Physically meaningful
fmax~5 GHz (from flawed U)Properly derived

12 Corrected Code

Replace the Y-parameter extraction in compute_fom() with proper complex reconstruction:

def compute_fom(sub):
    f   = sub["frequency"].values
    w   = 2 * np.pi * f                        # angular frequency

    # ✅ Reconstruct full complex Y = G + jωC
    Y11 = sub["a11"].values + 1j * w * sub["c11"].values
    Y12 = sub["a12"].values + 1j * w * sub["c12"].values
    Y21 = sub["a21"].values + 1j * w * sub["c21"].values
    Y22 = sub["a22"].values + 1j * w * sub["c22"].values

    # Everything downstream (S-params, H21, U) now works correctly
    S11, S12, S21, S22 = Y_to_S(Y11, Y12, Y21, Y22)
    # ... rest of function unchanged

With this one-line change (per Y-parameter), the full imaginary susceptance is included, H₂₁ will show the expected −20 dB/decade rolloff, and fT will be a meaningful GHz-range value.

Quick sanity check: At f = 1 GHz and VGS = 0 V, the imaginary part of Y₁₁ would be 2π × 10⁹ × 8.27 × 10⁻¹⁷ ≈ 5.2 × 10⁻⁷ S — that's three orders of magnitude larger than the real part a11 ≈ 2.4 × 10⁻¹² S. The imaginary part completely dominates, which is why omitting it is catastrophic.
